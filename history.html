Background

How the SDGs Came to Be

The 17 Sustainable Development Goals didn't appear overnight. They are the result of decades of global cooperation, debate, and hard lessons learned.

1992

The Earth Summit

World leaders gathered in Rio de Janeiro for the UN Conference on Environment and Development. For the first time, countries agreed that economic development and environmental protection had to go hand in hand. This planted the seed for what would eventually become the SDGs.

2000

The Millennium Development Goals

At the UN Millennium Summit, 189 countries signed the Millennium Declaration and agreed on 8 Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) to be achieved by 2015. These focused on reducing extreme poverty, improving health, and expanding education — but they only targeted developing countries and left out issues like climate and inequality.

2012

Rio+20 and the Call for Something Bigger

Twenty years after the first Earth Summit, world leaders met again in Rio. It was clear the MDGs weren't enough. Countries called for a new set of goals that would apply to every nation — rich or poor — and cover a much wider range of issues including climate, oceans, and inequality.

2013–2015

Three Years of Global Negotiation

An Open Working Group of 70 countries spent two years consulting governments, scientists, businesses, and ordinary people around the world. Over 8 million people participated in surveys. The result was a proposal for 17 goals and 169 targets — far more ambitious than the MDGs.

2015

The SDGs Are Born

On September 25, 2015, all 193 UN member states adopted the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development at a historic summit in New York. The 17 Sustainable Development Goals officially came into force on January 1, 2016. For the first time, every country in the world committed to the same set of goals.

2030

The Deadline

The world has until 2030 to achieve all 17 goals. Progress has been uneven — some goals are on track, others are falling behind, and events like the COVID-19 pandemic set things back significantly. But the goals remain the most widely agreed roadmap for a better world ever created.
